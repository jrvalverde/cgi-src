Studying the protein

The success of our analysis will depend on our interpretation of its results. The computer will apply a theoretical model to the data we feed it, and will provide us in return with a theoretical solution.

Since the theoretical approaches used are only approximations and make a number of shortcuts, so the results we get will come out as mere approximations and we must exercise care in accepting or rejecting them. The only way to be critic about the computer results is to know in advance as much about the protein we want to model as possible.

Finding out details about a protein relies mainly on experimental data, either found by ourselves or discovered by our fellow scientists. Most of this data will probably have been published in the Scientific Literature, and that's the first place to look for it. We can't possibly list all entry points to the scientific literature, mainly because most of it is not accessible on the WWW, but we do have some recommendations that we think make for good starting points:

  • Medline is the most popular database in Life Sciences. It started as an expanded electronic version of Index Medicus and covers most of the literature related (even remotely) to Life Sciences, together with abstracts and links to other sources of information.
  • PubMed Central is a digital archive of life sciences journal literature including full article contents
  • The Public Library of Science is a non-profit organization of scientists committed to making the world's scientific and medical literature freely accessible to scientists and to the public
  • PNAS has been one of the first Journals to make its contents freely available on the WWW shortly after publication.
  • The WWW Virtual Library is a broad resource of reference material on a wide number of scientific disciplines.

These were just a few starting points, there are much more, and you will surely know the most relevant ones in your field. Please note that these databases do not cover exhaustively all scientific knowledge, and may be sometimes outdated or contain errors. You are well advised to check out the results, contrast them with printed material and common wisdom and exercise rational criticism on anything reported in the literature. Blind gullibility is never good.

There is some data which is not vailable in the literature, or for which literary references are not available, too new, too old or no longer accessible. A lot of this data is available from other databases, oriented to specific experimental data. It is a good idea to look up these databases too, both generic and specialized databases on experimental scientific knowledge.

The wealth of experimental knowledge databases is so huge that we can't aspire to list it all here. Even if we could, there are new databases popping up all around every so often, and you are better qualified to know about them than we are.
